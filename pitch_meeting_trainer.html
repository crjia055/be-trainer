ZoomManaging the Call Itself
"Your line is a bit choppy / breaking up."
›
When to use
When the other person's audio is unstable — cutting in and out or distorted. More polite than 'I can't hear you'.
💡 'Choppy' = intermittent cutting. 'Breaking up' = signal deteriorating. Both are natural. Add: "Could you try turning off your video?" or "Let me try rejoining."
"There's a bit of an echo on your end."
›
When to use
When you can hear your own voice reflected back — usually because someone is not using headphones or is in a room with hard surfaces.
💡 Always say "on your end" — it's more tactful than implying it's their fault. Follow with: "You might want to switch to headphones."
"Let me pull up the slide deck."
›
When to use
Before sharing your screen to show a presentation. Natural and efficient — signals you're about to move to visual content.
Variations
"Let me pull up the materials."
"Let me share my screen — one second."
"I'm going to bring up the deck now."
💡 'Pull up' is the natural verb for retrieving something on screen. 'Slide deck' is universal — use it over 'PowerPoint' or 'presentation' for a more fluent tone.
"The lag is a bit significant."
›
When to use
When the video or audio is delayed — people are talking over each other or responses feel slow. A polite way to acknowledge the technical difficulty without blaming anyone.
💡 'Lag' is the technical word for delay. You can also say: "There seems to be a bit of a delay on the line" or "We're talking over each other — let's go one at a time."
"To circle back to your point…"
›
When to use
When you want to return to a point someone made earlier — either because it was left unaddressed or because new information has made it relevant again.
In a sentence
"To circle back to what you raised earlier about the timeline — I think the new data actually changes that picture."
💡 Shows you were listening and that nothing was dropped. Very effective for demonstrating attentiveness in a meeting.
"Let's give everyone a few minutes to jump on."
›
When to use
At the start of a call when you can see not everyone has joined yet — a polite way to hold the start without calling out specific latecomers.
💡 'Jump on' = join a call informally and quickly. More natural than 'join' in a Zoom context. You can also say: "We're still waiting for a couple of people — let's give it two minutes."
"Let's call it there." / "Let's wrap it up."
›
When to use
To bring a meeting to a close — either because you've covered everything or because time is up. Both are natural and professional.
Variations
"I think that's a good place to stop."
"Let's leave it there for today."
"I'm conscious of time — shall we wrap up?"
💡 'Call it there' is more decisive. 'Wrap it up' is slightly softer. Both work. Follow immediately with next steps or a summary to end professionally.
"I'll jump off / hop off now."
›
When to use
When you need to leave a call while others continue — a polite, informal way to signal your departure without disrupting the meeting.
In context
"I have another call starting — I'll hop off here. Thank you everyone."
"I'll jump off and let you two continue. I'll follow up by email."
💡 More natural than 'I'm going to leave now'. The informality of 'jump/hop off' matches the Zoom context well. Always add a reason or a handoff.
"I'll shoot you an invite."
›
When to use
When you've agreed to schedule a follow-up call or meeting — you're confirming you'll send the calendar invitation.
Variations
"I'll send over a calendar invite."
"I'll get a meeting on the calendar."
"Expect an invite from me shortly."
💡 'Shoot' here means to send quickly and informally. Very common in North American and increasingly global business English. More energetic than 'I'll send you an invitation'.
OpeningStarting a Client Conversation
"How has the recent volatility in the tech sector been treating your team?"
›
When to use
Opening small talk with a client in the tech sector during a period of market movement. Shows you are informed about their world before the meeting starts.
💡 'How has X been treating you/your team?' is a warm, slightly idiomatic way to ask 'how are you dealing with X?' It implies the market is an external force acting on them — empathetic framing.
"I've been following your company's recent expansion into Southeast Asia — it's quite an impressive move."
›
When to use
Opening by referencing something specific and recent about the client's business — shows you've done your homework before the call.
💡 The most powerful openings reference something specific — a recent deal, a press release, a strategic move. Generic compliments land flat; specific ones land as genuine attention. Always have one ready before a client call.
"It's been a whirlwind of a week for the markets — I'm sure you're glad it's finally Friday."
›
When to use
Light Friday small talk that acknowledges shared market turbulence — creates immediate common ground without requiring the client to share anything personal.
Key phrase
"Whirlwind" = a period of intense, fast-moving activity. "It's been a whirlwind" is very natural for describing a hectic period.
💡 Safe, warm, and universal. Works for any market professional on a Friday after a volatile week. The key is that you're acknowledging their world, not making small talk about yours.
"I know your schedule is likely packed."
›
When to use
Acknowledging the client's time before getting to the point — shows respect for their schedule and signals you won't waste time.
Variations
"I know you're pressed for time, so I'll keep this focused."
"I won't take up more time than we need."
💡 Very effective as a bridge to getting started efficiently. It flatters the client (their time is valuable) and signals you are organised and respectful. Always follow it immediately with your first substantive point.
"How has your week been shaping up so far? I imagine it's been busy with the Q1 wrap-up."
›
When to use
Warm opening that shows awareness of the client's calendar — acknowledges what they're probably dealing with at that particular moment in the year.
💡 'How has your week been shaping up?' is more sophisticated than 'How's your week?' — it implies the week is still forming, still in progress. Always add a specific reference ('Q1 wrap-up', 'the rate decision', 'post-results') to show you know their world.
"It's great to finally connect in person / on camera. I've been looking forward to this deep dive."
›
When to use
When meeting someone for the first time after prior email/phone contact, or reconnecting after a long gap. Sets an energised, engaged tone from the start.
Key phrase
"Deep dive" = a thorough, detailed exploration of a topic. Signals that this meeting has substance and you're prepared for real engagement.
💡 'Finally connect' acknowledges the lead-up and creates warmth. 'Deep dive' sets the expectation that this will be a substantive conversation — not just surface-level. Effective for first proper strategy sessions.
BridgeMoving from Small Talk to Business
"That sounds like a great way to recharge. Speaking of energy and focus, I want to be respectful of your time — so let's cut to the chase and drill down into the strategy."
›
When to use
After two or three minutes of opening small talk — the bridge that pivots gracefully from personal conversation to business substance.
Key techniques
1. Acknowledge what they said: "That sounds like a great way to recharge."
2. Link word: "Speaking of energy and focus…"
3. Respect signal: "I want to be respectful of your time."
4. Transition: "Let's cut to the chase and drill down."
💡 The bridge is a skill most people underuse. Going from 'great, the kids are good' directly to 'so here's the pitch' is jarring. A bridge acknowledges the personal moment and pivots to business in one smooth move. 'Speaking of…' is the classic linking device.
ClosingEnding a Meeting and Securing Next Steps
"How does this approach align with the internal discussions you've been having lately?"
›
When to use
Closing question that invites the client to compare your proposal with their own internal thinking — a soft check on fit without asking 'do you like it?'
💡 Far more sophisticated than 'What do you think?' It presupposes internal discussions are happening (flattery) and invites them to reveal their internal framing — which is valuable intelligence.
"Based on what we've covered, is there a specific area you'd like us to drill down into further?"
›
When to use
Closing question that gives the client agency — inviting them to direct the next conversation based on what resonated.
💡 'Drill down further' signals depth and willingness to go beyond the surface. The question also tells you what they care about most — the area they name is the one worth building the next meeting around.
"Are there any specific concerns we should keep on our radar?"
›
When to use
An open invitation for the client to flag anything they're worried about — framed generously ('our radar') so they don't feel they're criticising you.
💡 'Keep on our radar' = to monitor actively. The phrase frames you as a partner tracking their concerns alongside them — not a vendor defending a proposal. Very effective for surfacing hidden objections.
"To keep the momentum going, what would be the best way to socialise this with your team?"
›
When to use
Closing question that assumes progress and asks how to spread the idea internally — 'socialise' here means share informally to build internal support.
Key word
"Socialise" (in business English) = to informally share an idea with colleagues to build buy-in before a formal decision. NOT about social events.
💡 'Keep the momentum going' assumes forward motion — a subtle but powerful presupposition. 'Socialise this' is a highly professional phrase that signals awareness of corporate politics and internal process.
"We aren't just looking to close this deal — we want to be your strategic partner for the long haul."
›
When to use
Closing statement that repositions the relationship from transactional to strategic — signals long-term commitment rather than fee-hunting.
💡 'Long haul' = the long term, with a sense of endurance and commitment. This phrase works because it explicitly names what the client fears (a bank chasing a fee) and rejects it. Only say it if you mean it.
"Feel free to use me as a sounding board for these ideas."
›
When to use
Offering yourself as an informal resource for thinking out loud — before a formal mandate, this builds the relationship by offering value without strings.
Key phrase
"Sounding board" = a person you can think out loud with to test ideas before committing to them. An invaluable role to occupy with a client.
💡 Being a sounding board is one of the most valuable things an advisor can offer — it costs nothing and builds the deepest trust. The phrase signals availability and intellectual engagement, not just service delivery.
"Our goal is to be a seamless extension of your team, helping you navigate any snags along the way."
›
When to use
Closing statement that defines the partnership as deeply integrated — not an external vendor, but an extension of their own capability.
Key words
"Seamless" = without friction, invisible join. "Extension of your team" = operating as insiders, not outsiders. "Navigate snags" = handle the unexpected obstacles.
💡 Three very precise words in one sentence. 'Seamless' is the aspirational standard; 'extension' removes the us-and-them dynamic; 'snags' is honest about the fact that problems will arise. Together they paint a complete partnership picture.
PricingDiscussing Terms and Structure
"I'm happy to socialise a lower coupon with the investors — but in exchange, they may need a lower premium."
›
When to use
In CB pricing discussions when the issuer wants to lower the coupon — flagging to the client that the trade-off is a lower conversion premium.
💡 'Socialise' here = test informally with investors before committing. The sentence structure 'I'm happy to X, but in exchange Y' is a clean way to present a trade-off without saying 'no' to either side.
"The lack of a 'change of control' protection is a non-starter for our anchor investors."
›
When to use
When a structural feature the issuer wants to drop is one that CB investors consider essential — conveying a firm limit from the investor base.
💡 'Non-starter' = something that cannot even be the beginning of a conversation. Very direct and final. Use it sparingly and only when you genuinely mean it — it shuts a door and needs to stay shut.
"If the book isn't building as fast as we'd like, we may need to sweeten the deal with a slightly higher yield."
›
When to use
During bookbuild when demand is slower than expected — preparing the issuer for a potential pricing adjustment before it becomes a crisis.
💡 'Sweeten the deal' = make the offer more attractive to buyers. 'Book isn't building' = investor orders are not accumulating at the required pace. Both are very natural in live deal execution.
"We need to manage the optics of this issuance — ensure it's seen as a strategic growth move, not a 'lender of last resort' situation."
›
When to use
When a company is issuing debt or equity under potentially ambiguous circumstances — flagging that the narrative management around the deal is as important as the pricing.
Key phrase
"Manage the optics" = control how something appears to the outside world. Not just what it is, but what it looks like.
💡 'Optics' is now standard in finance — it means the perception or appearance of something, separately from the reality. 'Lender of last resort' signals desperation. Naming the risk explicitly in conversation is what allows you to prevent it.
TimelineManaging Windows and Urgency
"There is a clear window of opportunity right now — we should capture the early-mover advantage before the macro environment shifts."
›
When to use
When market conditions are temporarily favourable — making the case for acting now rather than waiting.
Key phrases
"Window of opportunity" = a period when conditions are right, but it won't last.
"Early-mover advantage" = benefit of acting before others do, while conditions are still optimal.
💡 Both phrases create legitimate urgency without being pushy. The argument is structural (conditions are temporarily good) not personal (you should do this for our fee).
"It's time to strike while the iron is hot and initiate the workstream."
›
When to use
More forceful version of the urgency argument — combining the classic idiom with the actionable next step ('initiate the workstream').
Key phrase
"Strike while the iron is hot" = act at the optimal moment, before conditions change. From blacksmithing — metal is most workable when hot.
💡 The idiom is well-known but still effective. 'Initiate the workstream' is the critical addition — it moves immediately from the argument to the action, which prevents the client from agreeing in principle but delaying in practice.
"If we can square away the mandates by Friday, we'll be pacing toward a launch in the first week of next month."
›
When to use
When trying to confirm a specific decision by a specific date in order to hit a target launch window.
Key phrases
"Square away" = finalise, settle, get it done cleanly.
"Pacing toward" = on track to reach, moving at the right speed.
💡 Both 'square away' and 'pacing toward' are very high-frequency in execution contexts. They make the timeline feel manageable and jointly owned — not a deadline being imposed, but a shared plan being executed.
"Now that we've found a middle ground, I'm excited to pivot our focus toward the execution."
›
When to use
After a negotiation has resolved — closing the terms discussion and redirecting energy toward making the deal happen.
💡 'Found a middle ground' acknowledges the negotiation without dwelling on it. 'Excited to pivot our focus' signals forward momentum. The combination closes one chapter and opens the next in a single sentence.
PitchSelling the Bank and the Deal
"Our desk is reading the tape in real-time — we know exactly when the market is receptive and when to wait."
›
When to use
Differentiating on market timing expertise — arguing that the bank's real-time market intelligence is a competitive advantage.
Key phrase
"Reading the tape" = monitoring live market data and flow to interpret current conditions. A trading-floor expression meaning real-time market intelligence.
💡 'Reading the tape' is a credibility signal — it implies active, experienced market surveillance, not just modelled projections. Very effective in pitches for time-sensitive transactions.
"We don't just execute — we manage the narrative. We ensure the equity story is framed in a way that resonates with both credit and equity investors."
›
When to use
Differentiating on more than mechanical execution — arguing that strategic communication around the deal is equally important.
💡 'Manage the narrative' = control how the deal story is perceived and communicated. 'Resonates' is a powerful word — it implies the message doesn't just reach investors but creates an emotional and analytical alignment. The distinction between 'just execute' and 'manage the narrative' is the core of this pitch.
"We have skin in the game right alongside you."
›
When to use
Demonstrating shared risk — the bank is not just a fee-collector but has genuine exposure to the outcome.
Stronger version
"We are so confident in the pricing of this CB that we are willing to provide a hard underwrite — we're putting our own balance sheet on the line. That's how much skin in the game we have."
💡 'Skin in the game' = having a personal stake in the outcome. Originally from gambling — you can't win if you haven't bet. In finance: the bank is risking its own capital, which aligns its incentives fully with the client's. One of the most powerful credibility statements in a pitch.
"This deal is top-of-mind for our senior leadership."
›
When to use
Signalling that the deal has visibility and attention at the highest levels of the organisation — the client is not just a number.
💡 'Top-of-mind' = the most immediate, active concern — something being thought about right now. The phrase signals priority without overpromising. Clients want to know senior people know their name.
"We aren't looking for a quick fee — we want to support your capital structure for the long haul, from this issuance through to the final refinancing."
›
When to use
Differentiating on long-term commitment — explicitly rejecting the transactional bank model in favour of a lifecycle relationship.
💡 'Quick fee' names exactly what clients fear and rejects it. 'From this issuance through to the final refinancing' paints a specific long arc — not vague 'partnership' language but a concrete commitment across the lifecycle of their capital structure. The specificity is what makes it credible.
BanksInter-Bank / Syndicate Coordination
"Let's calibrate our views against the recent comps to ensure we aren't misreading the appetite."
›
When to use
Before a joint pricing call — checking that the co-leads are using the same reference points before going to market.
💡 'Calibrate' = adjust and align to a common standard. 'Comps' = comparable transactions. 'Appetite' = investor demand. All three are essential syndicate vocabulary.
"We need to speak with one voice during the pricing call this afternoon."
›
When to use
Before a joint client call when multiple banks need to present a unified position — ensuring no contradictions or gaps are visible to the client.
💡 'Speak with one voice' is one of the most important phrases in syndication. Disagreements between banks must be resolved before the client hears anything. The call is not the place to discover you have different views.
"Let's take this offline — have our respective heads of syndicate hash it out."
›
When to use
When a disagreement between banks in a meeting is going in circles — proposing to remove it from the current conversation and resolve it bilaterally.
Key phrase
"Hash it out" = to have a detailed, sometimes difficult conversation until a resolution is reached. Implies genuine effort to work through to an answer.
💡 'Take this offline' = handle it outside the current meeting, privately. Essential skill in multi-bank settings — you never want clients to see banks disagreeing in front of them.
"Let's strip away the noise — the primary objective is execution certainty. If we argue over 5 bps and lose the window, we all lose."
›
When to use
When inter-bank debate is getting lost in detail — a forceful refocus on what actually matters for the deal to succeed.
💡 'Strip away the noise' = remove everything irrelevant and focus on what is essential. 'We all lose' makes the stakes shared — not a threat to one bank but a reminder of the collective interest. Very effective for breaking deadlocks.
"If we triangulate the feedback from our respective desks, the sweet spot is closer to a 2.75% coupon."
›
When to use
Synthesising feedback from multiple sources to arrive at a market-clearing price point — presenting the consensus as a triangulated conclusion.
Key phrases
"Triangulate" = combine multiple data points to locate a more accurate answer than any single source provides.
"Sweet spot" = the optimal point that balances competing requirements.
💡 'Triangulate' sounds analytical and rigorous — it elevates the conversation from opinion to method. 'Sweet spot' is specific enough to imply precision without locking in a single number.
"We should stress-test the assumption that the market will absorb a 7-year maturity — our desk is seeing more traction in the 5-year space."
›
When to use
Challenging a co-lead's assumption about deal structure based on your own market intelligence — raising a potential risk constructively.
💡 'Stress-test the assumption' = rigorously examine whether the assumption holds under adverse conditions. It doesn't say 'you're wrong' — it says 'let's make sure we're right'. Diplomatic and precise.
