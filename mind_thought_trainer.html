Latin Root
EGO
Self — the sense of "I"
▾ expand
Egoist
noun
A person who believes their own interests are the most important — operates from pure self-interest.
"He's a total egoist; he only cares about what's in it for him."
Egotist
noun
A person who talks excessively about themselves and their own achievements — self-obsessed in conversation.
"Talking to that egotist is exhausting — he only talks about his own success."
Ego
noun
One's sense of self-importance and self-worth. A healthy ego is positive; a fragile or inflated ego causes problems.
"Winning that competition really gave his ego a huge boost."
Egocentric
adjective
Seeing the world only from one's own perspective — unable to genuinely consider others' viewpoints or feelings.
"Small children are naturally egocentric and don't realise others have feelings too."
Egomaniac
noun
A person with an obsessive, almost pathological belief in their own importance — beyond egotism into something dangerous.
"The CEO was such an egomaniac that he wouldn't listen to any advice from his team."
Egomaniacal
adjective
describes behaviour
Characteristic of an egomaniac — driven by an obsessive need for control and admiration.
"His egomaniacal behaviour eventually drove all his friends away."
The spectrum: The egoist is self-interested (obnoxious), the egotist talks too much about themselves (tiresome), the egocentric cannot see others' perspectives (intolerable), and the egomaniac is obsessively self-important — potentially dangerous and slightly mad. Each step on this spectrum is more severe than the last.
Latin Root
ALTER
Other — the other one, another
▾ expand
Altruist
noun
A person who puts others' wellbeing above their own — the opposite of an egoist.
"As a true altruist, she spent her entire inheritance building a local hospital."
Altruism
noun
Selfless concern for others; the practice of giving without expectation of return.
"The organisation relies on the altruism of everyday people to keep running."
Altruistic
adjective
Acting out of concern for others rather than self-interest.
"It was an altruistic move to give up his seat for the elderly lady."
Alteration
noun
A change or modification — making something different from what it was.
"The dress is perfect, but it needs a small alteration in the sleeves."
Altercation
noun
formal/legal tone
A noisy, heated argument or disagreement — more formal than 'argument', often implying a public scene.
"The two drivers had a loud altercation over a parking spot."
Alter ego
noun phrase
A second self — a different version of oneself, often a persona adopted in a specific context.
"By day he's a shy accountant, but on stage his alter ego is a rock star."
Alternate
adj / verb
Every other one (adj); to take turns or switch between two things (verb).
"We should alternate who picks the music so everyone gets a turn."
Alternative
noun / adj
A different option; another available choice.
"Is there an alternative to surgery, like physical therapy?"
Key distinction: Altruism comes from alter (other) — it literally means orientating yourself toward the other person. The opposite is not 'selfishness' but rather egoism — both words get their meaning from which direction (self vs other) your concern flows.
Latin Root
VERTO
To turn — to face inward or outward
▾ expand
Introvert
noun / adj
A person who is energised by solitude and internal thought — social interaction drains rather than recharges them.
"I'm an introvert, so I usually need a quiet night at home after a busy work week."
Extrovert
noun / adj
A person energised by social interaction — they gain energy from being around others.
"My sister is an extrovert who feels energised by being in large crowds."
Ambivert
noun
A person who falls between introvert and extrovert — comfortable in both social and solitary situations.
"I'm an ambivert; I love parties, but I also value my alone time."
The root meaning: Intro (inward) + verto (turn) = turned inward. Extra (outward) + verto = turned outward. Ambi (both) = turned both ways. The energy model, not the shyness model, is the most accurate way to use these words.
Latin / French Roots
DEXTER · GAUCHE · ADROIT
Skill, dexterity, social grace — or the lack of it
▾ expand
Dexterity
noun
Skill and precision in using one's hands — or, more broadly, mental agility and nimbleness.
"Playing video games at a high level requires a lot of manual dexterity."
Dexterous
adjective
Skilful, precise, and nimble — with hands or in thinking.
"The surgeon was so dexterous that the operation was finished in record time."
Ambidextrous
adjective
ambi = both
Able to use both hands with equal skill — or, metaphorically, comfortable in two very different roles.
"She is ambidextrous, so she can write with both hands simultaneously."
Gauche
adjective
from French: left-handed
Socially awkward or tactless — lacking grace or awareness in social situations. The French word for 'left' carries the association of clumsiness.
"It was a bit gauche of him to ask how much money I make at a dinner party."
Gaucherie
noun
A socially awkward act or remark — an instance of gauche behaviour.
"His social gaucherie made the whole room feel awkward."
Adroit
adjective
from French: right-handed
Clever, skilled, and nimble — especially in handling difficult situations smoothly. The opposite of gauche.
"The politician was admired for his adroitness in avoiding difficult questions."
Adroitness
noun
The quality of being adroit — clever, smooth handling of a situation.
"She navigated the difficult negotiation with remarkable adroitness."
Etymology worth knowing: In French, 'droit' = right (direction), 'gauche' = left. In most European languages, right-handedness was associated with skill and correctness; left with awkwardness. That's why 'gauche' means socially clumsy and 'adroit' means skilful — both literally just mean 'left-handed' and 'right-handed' in French.

Synonyms of gauche: clumsy, tactless, bumbling. Synonyms of adroit: ingenious, nimble, dexterous.
Greek Root
MISEIN · ANTHROPE · GYNE · GAMOS
To hate · Mankind · Woman · Marriage
▾ expand
Misanthrope
noun
A person who dislikes and distrusts people in general — tends to avoid human company.
"The old man was a bit of a misanthrope and hated when neighbours walked near his lawn."
Misogynist
noun / adj
miso + gyne (woman)
A person who harbours prejudice or hostility toward women.
"The manager was fired for making misogynist remarks about his female employees."
Philanthropist
noun
philos (love) + anthrope
A person who promotes human welfare through generous charitable donations and action.
"The philanthropist donated millions to help find a cure for cancer."
Philanthropy
noun
The practice of donating time, money, or resources to benefit society — love of mankind in action.
"The university was built through the philanthropy of a local businessman."
Philanthropic
adjective
Relating to or characterised by philanthropy.
"The company has a philanthropic mission to provide clean water globally."
Monogamy
noun
mono (one) + gamos
The practice of having only one partner at a time.
"Many people believe that monogamy is the best foundation for a stable family."
Polygamy
noun
poly (many) + gamos
The practice of having more than one spouse simultaneously.
"In some cultures, polygamy is a legal and traditional practice."
Bigamy
noun
bi (two) + gamos
The crime of marrying someone while already legally married to another person.
"He was charged with bigamy because he was still married to his first wife."
The miso- prefix: miso + anthrope = misanthrope (hates people); miso + gyne = misogynist (hates women); miso + gamos = misogamist (hates marriage). Once you know the prefix, the meaning is always predictable.

Philanthropy in professional use: In finance and business, 'philanthropic', 'philanthropist', and 'philanthropy' appear constantly in ESG contexts, foundation work, family office discussions, and corporate social responsibility. These three forms are worth having at your fingertips.
